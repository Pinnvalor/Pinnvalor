Evaluating Business Growth with Precision: CAGR, PEG Ratio, and Retention Rate Explained

In today’s data-driven business environment, measuring growth is no longer just about tracking revenue increases. True financial insight lies in understanding how growth is achieved, how sustainable it is, and whether it creates long-term value.

This is where key growth ratios—CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate), PEG Ratio, and Retention Rate—come into play. Together, these metrics provide a comprehensive framework for evaluating business performance, investment potential, and strategic direction.

Do your numbers indicate true performance or misleading trends?

Fast growth may look impressive, but sustainable growth builds lasting value. The difference lies in how you measure it.

Why Growth Ratios Matter

Growth ratios go beyond surface-level numbers. They help answer critical questions:

  • Is the company growing consistently or erratically?
  • Is the stock fairly valued relative to its growth?
  • Is the business retaining earnings effectively for future expansion?

1. CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate): Measuring Consistent Growth

What is CAGR?
CAGR represents the annualized growth rate of an investment or business metric over a specific period, assuming profits are reinvested.

Formula:
CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) - 1

Why CAGR is Important:

  • Smooths out volatility to show consistent growth
  • Enables comparison across companies or investments
  • Useful for long-term performance evaluation

Example:
If a company’s revenue grows from ₹10 crore to ₹20 crore in 5 years, CAGR reflects the steady growth rate rather than year-to-year fluctuations.

Limitations:

  • Ignores interim volatility
  • Assumes constant growth, which is rarely realistic

2. PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings to Growth): Linking Value with Growth

What is the PEG Ratio?
The PEG ratio refines the traditional P/E ratio by incorporating earnings growth, offering a clearer picture of whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued.

Formula:
PEG = P/E Ratio / Earnings Growth Rate

Interpretation:

  • PEG < 1 → Potentially undervalued
  • PEG = 1 → Fairly valued
  • PEG > 1 → Possibly overvalued

Why PEG Matters:

  • Balances valuation with growth expectations
  • Helps investors identify high-growth opportunities at reasonable prices
  • Useful in comparing companies within the same industry

Example:
A company with a P/E of 20 and growth rate of 25% has a PEG of 0.8—indicating potential undervaluation.

Limitations:

  • Relies on projected growth, which may be uncertain
  • Less effective for companies with inconsistent earnings

3. Retention Rate: Fueling Sustainable Growth

What is Retention Rate?
Retention rate (also called the plowback ratio) measures the proportion of earnings retained within the business instead of being distributed as dividends.

Formula:
Retention Rate = 1 - Dividend Payout Ratio

Why It Matters:

  • Indicates how much profit is reinvested for growth
  • Essential for funding expansion without external financing
  • Directly impacts future earnings potential

Example:
If a company retains 70% of its earnings, it has more internal resources to invest in innovation, expansion, or debt reduction.

Connection to Growth:
Sustainable Growth Rate (SGR) = ROE × Retention Rate

Limitations:

  • High retention doesn’t always mean efficient use of funds
  • Must be analyzed alongside return metrics like ROE

Integrating the Three Ratios for Holistic Analysis

Individually, each ratio offers valuable insight. Together, they create a powerful framework:

  • CAGR: Consistency of performance
  • PEG Ratio: Growth-adjusted valuation
  • Retention Rate: Sustainability of growth

Combined Insight:

  • High CAGR + Low PEG + Strong Retention → Ideal growth company
  • High CAGR + High PEG → Growth may already be priced in
  • Low Retention + High Growth → Growth may not be sustainable

Practical Applications

For Investors:

  • Identify undervalued growth stocks
  • Compare companies across sectors
  • Make long-term portfolio decisions

For Management:

  • Evaluate internal growth strategies
  • Optimize dividend vs reinvestment decisions
  • Align financial policies with long-term goals

For Analysts:

  • Build robust valuation models
  • Assess business sustainability
  • Provide data-driven recommendations

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Relying on a single metric without context
  • Ignoring industry benchmarks
  • Overestimating growth projections in PEG
  • Misinterpreting high retention as efficient reinvestment

Conclusion

In an era where precision drives performance, understanding growth requires more than just tracking numbers—it demands context, comparison, and strategic interpretation.

CAGR reveals the consistency of growth, PEG Ratio aligns valuation with future potential, and Retention Rate highlights the engine powering sustainable expansion.

When used together, these metrics empower businesses, investors, and analysts to move beyond assumptions and make well-informed, forward-looking decisions.
