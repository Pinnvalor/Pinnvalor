üîç What is Other Comprehensive Income?

Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) refers to revenues, expenses, gains, and losses that are excluded from net income on the income statement. Instead, they are reported separately under equity in the balance sheet, flowing through the Statement of Comprehensive Income.

Unlike regular income, OCI items are typically unrealized, non-operational, and not part of day-to-day business activities. Still, they can significantly influence a company's financial position and investor perception.

üßæ Common Components of OCI

  • Foreign Currency Translation Adjustments: Exchange rate differences from translating foreign subsidiaries are included in OCI.
  • Unrealized Gains/Losses on Debt Securities (AFS): Fair value changes in debt instruments classified as Fair Value through OCI (FVOCI).
  • Remeasurements of Defined Benefit Plans: Actuarial gains/losses on pensions and employee benefits are recognized here.
  • Cash Flow Hedges: Effective portions of gains/losses on hedging instruments are recorded in OCI until the hedged item impacts profit/loss.

üß† Why OCI Matters

  • Enhanced Transparency: Offers insights into economic events impacting the company without distorting net earnings.
  • Improved Comparability: Helps compare core operational performance across companies and periods.
  • Impact on Equity: Although it doesn‚Äôt affect net income, OCI impacts shareholders‚Äô equity through Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (AOCI).

As per Ind AS 1 / IAS 1 ‚Äì Presentation of Financial Statements, OCI is reported in the Statement of Profit and Loss and Other Comprehensive Income. It may be presented:

Each OCI item should be presented net of tax or with tax effects disclosed.
